## The hook

If you grew up watching football in a language that is not English, or you work in sports broadcasting and you have an ear for the terrace voice of a specific country, this post is for you.

The Tournamental renderer plays the 2022 World Cup Final with full commentary, 200 minutes long, in ten languages. Every one of those languages was machine-translated from the English master script and seeded with my best guess at sports clichés.

I pronounce "Mbappé" wrong in three of them. Come and fix it. Drips will pay you a slice on every paid replay-stream view that touches your work.

This post is the long version. The pot-of-gold-or-pot-of-shit manifesto at [/blog/2026-05-12-pot-of-gold-or-pot-of-shit/](/blog/2026-05-12-pot-of-gold-or-pot-of-shit/) is the broader open-source case; this post is the narrow call to multilingual sports fans. The rest covers what I built, what is wrong, what an expert could ship in a day, a week, or a month, and how Drips routes revenue to you in USDC.

## What I built so far

The commentary pipeline produces a full live-broadcast voice over the Argentina versus France 2022 final, synchronised to the StatsBomb event timeline, in ten languages. The source assets live at [`data/commentary/wc2022-final-arg-fra/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/data/commentary/wc2022-final-arg-fra) and the file structure is one Markdown file per language, plus a `manifest.json` that maps each utterance to a timestamp and an event ID.

### The file structure

```
data/commentary/wc2022-final-arg-fra/
├── manifest.json
├── ar.md        Arabic
├── de.md        German
├── en.md        English (master)
├── es.md        Spanish
├── fr.md        French
├── it.md        Italian
├── ja.md        Japanese
├── pt.md        Portuguese
├── ru.md        Russian
└── zh.md        Mandarin Chinese
```

### The English master

The English master is the long-form version: around 2,972 lines, 200 minutes of audio at conversational pace, with crowd-reaction cues and inflection markers. The script is written to sound like a real commentary partnership, two voices passing back and forth, not a single robot. It was authored by a human (me) with heavy editorial passes from a language-model agent that ran against a sports-commentary corpus.

The script structure is, per utterance:

```
## minute 23:18, period 1
[crowd: rising murmur]
LEAD: Messi steps up to the spot.
COLOUR: He has scored four from four in this tournament from twelve yards.
LEAD: Pace, pace, pace. Lloris dives left. Messi places it right.
[crowd: roar]
LEAD: One-nil Argentina.
COLOUR: Lionel Messi opens the scoring in the World Cup Final.
```

The `[crowd: ...]` cues drive the ducking on the crowd loop. The `LEAD:` and `COLOUR:` prefixes route to the two TTS voices. The markdown headings drive the event-time synchronisation; the manifest maps each heading to a StatsBomb event ID.

### The other nine languages

The other nine languages were generated from the English master via a structured translation pipeline. The doc that explains the pipeline is [docs/31-live-commentary-overlay.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/31-live-commentary-overlay.md). The renderer integration is in [`apps/web/app/api/commentary/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/web/app/api/commentary). The text-to-speech pipeline uses [ElevenLabs](https://elevenlabs.io/) per-language voice models, with each language anchored to a single voice for the main commentator and a second voice for the colour commentator. The voice mapping is in `manifest.json` as `voice_main` and `voice_colour` keys per language.

### The translation pipeline, in detail

The pipeline is:

1. **DeepL initial pass** on the full English master. [DeepL](https://www.deepl.com/) handles the structural translation well across all ten target languages.
2. **Language-model rewrite** with sports-cliche templates inserted. The templates are per-language and live at `data/commentary/templates/<lang>/cliche-set.md`. The LLM reads the DeepL output, identifies the cliché slots, and replaces the literal translation with the local-language cliché where appropriate.
3. **Manual review** for the four languages where I have a friend who could spare an hour (German, Italian, French, Russian). The other five (Spanish, Portuguese, Arabic, Japanese, Mandarin) ship with only the LLM rewrite.
4. **Per-name SSML pronunciation pass** on the player roster. This is the step I have not done; it is documented in the doc but not implemented.
5. **TTS render** via ElevenLabs API, with per-language voice mapping.

The pipeline is end-to-end. It runs as a build script at `data/commentary/scripts/build-pipeline.mjs`. A single run takes about forty minutes for the full 200-minute match across ten languages, mostly ElevenLabs synthesis time.

### What this means for a viewer

What this means in practice: a French fan watching the AR-FR replay hears a French commentary track over a French-language HUD with the right player names, the right club references, and the right rhetorical pacing on a goal call. A Mandarin fan gets a Mandarin track. An Arabic fan gets an Arabic track. The infrastructure works. The renderer is multilingual from launch.

The English track sounds, mostly, like a real commentary partnership.

The other nine tracks sound, mostly, like a competent translation of a real commentary partnership. Which is not the same thing.

## What I cannot do alone

### I speak two languages, badly

I am a New Zealander. I speak English natively. I speak a passable, vacation-grade Spanish. I can read enough French to follow a Le Monde sports article but I would never claim to write commentary in it. The other seven languages are languages I cannot speak.

### The pipeline is automation, not authorship

I used machine translation seeded with sports-cliche templates from the [Wikipedia football phrasebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_association_football_terms) and a curated list of [common football commentary idioms](https://www.theguardian.com/football/2014/jul/15/world-cup-football-cliches). The pipeline goes English master → DeepL initial pass → language-model rewrite with the cliche templates inserted → human review (for the languages where I had a friend who could spare an hour). The five languages where I did not have a friend to ask got the first two stages and went live as-is.

This means several things will be wrong, and I want to be specific about them so you know exactly what to fix.

### What is wrong, by language

The **Portuguese** version uses Continental Portuguese registry by default. The Brazilian Portuguese expansion (Tournamental's biggest growth market in 2026) will sound off to anyone watching from São Paulo or Rio. The terrace voice, the syllable timing, the use of "rapaz" versus "cara", the rhythm of the goal call (Brazilians extend "goooooooooooooooooooooooooooool" for a reason, and the cadence varies by club tradition), none of this is right in the current script.

The **Spanish** version uses a neutral Latin-American registry that is recognisable in Mexico and Colombia but reads slightly wrong in Argentina, Spain, Chile, and Uruguay. The Argentina-vs-France final is the worst possible match to ship with a non-Argentine Spanish track. The "che" idioms, the Buenos Aires terrace songs, the Messi-specific pet names ("Lio", "la Pulga"), the way an Argentine commentator builds tension in the run-up to a Messi free kick: all of this is in the cultural air of Argentine commentary and none of it is in our script.

The **Arabic** version is the one I am most uncertain about. The pipeline translated everything into Modern Standard Arabic because that is the registry the LLM defaults to. Real football commentary in the Arabic-speaking world is overwhelmingly **regional**: Egyptian Arabic dominates north African audiences, Gulf Arabic dominates the GCC, Levantine Arabic dominates the Levant. The Modern Standard Arabic version will sound to an Egyptian fan the way the BBC's received-pronunciation news reader would sound to a Liverpool terrace.

The **Japanese** version has formality-register problems. Sports commentary in Japan switches between polite (-masu) and casual (-da) forms based on emotional intensity, with a specific rhetorical move where the commentator drops into casual form on a goal as a marker of authenticity. The current script uses polite form throughout. A native Japanese speaker will read it as flat.

The **Mandarin** version has the inverse problem. Mandarin sports commentary uses a specific high-energy register that the LLM defaulted away from because it sounded "exaggerated" in the source corpus. The current script is too restrained. The goal calls do not crescendo.

The **Korean** version does not yet ship. The K-League audience is large and growing, and Korean broadcast convention uses a specific high-energy goal call that the LLM defaulted away from. Korean is one of the languages on the "we should ship this next" list.

The **Hindi** version does not yet ship. The IPL audience expansion is on the roadmap and Hindi commentary is a market we need to enter before the cricket-season expansion lands.

The **Bengali** version does not yet ship. Bangladesh has one of the most football-passionate populations in the world relative to per-capita GDP and the Bengali audience is underserved by global sports media. We need this.

The **German**, **Italian**, **French**, and **Russian** versions are the four I have native or near-native friends for. They got the pipeline plus one round of human review. They are passable. They are not great. The German is too formal for a Bundesrepublik football audience. The Italian is too neutral for any of the [three traditional Italian commentary schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Football_in_Italy#Football_in_Italian_media). The French is fine, mostly, except for the player-name pronunciations where the TTS voice is mispronouncing "Mbappé" by stressing the second syllable. The Russian is fine, mostly, except that the post-Crimea generation of Russian football commentary has shifted in ways my friend was not in a position to brief me on.

### Languages we have not yet shipped

And there are languages **we have not yet shipped** that we should. Bengali for the IPL audience expansion. Hindi for the same. Urdu for the Pakistan cricket audience. Swahili for East African football. Yoruba and Hausa for West African football. Korean for the K-League and the AFC Asian Cup audience. Vietnamese for Southeast Asia. Indonesian. Filipino. Each one of those audiences is a market that Tournamental wants to reach and currently cannot, because the commentary track is monolingual at the level of "language we shipped".

### Pronunciation issues across the stack

The **machine-pronunciation issues** in the TTS voices are a separate problem. ElevenLabs handles English names well in most voice models. It struggles with non-English names in the wrong-language context: "Mbappé" pronounced in a French voice is fine, in an English voice it lands on the wrong syllable, in a Japanese voice it loses the accented é entirely. The fix is per-name SSML hints in the manifest, and I have not done the work to author them.

The 22-starter squad list for the AR-FR final lives at [`apps/statsbomb-replay/data/wc2022-final-players.csv`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/apps/statsbomb-replay/data/wc2022-final-players.csv) with the full name, jersey number, and club. The right SSML override would be a `<phoneme>` tag per name per voice, mapped through a lookup table in `data/commentary/pronunciations/<lang>/players.json`. None of these files exist yet.

### The crowd-mix issue

The crowd loop, the way it interacts with the commentary, is the next layer below the language registry. Brazilian crowds chant differently than Argentine crowds. The current crowd loop is one BBC sound-archive WAV that ducks under the commentary cue. A localised pass would author per-region crowd loops: Argentine for the Argentina-supporters end, French for the France-supporters end, with mixed crowd elsewhere. The mix would crossfade based on camera position.

This is a translator-with-audio-experience job. The audio assets need to be sourced (the BBC's CC-BY archive is one source; field recordings from terrace songs at amateur matches are another). The integration is in `apps/web/lib/sound/crowd.ts`. Today the file is empty.

### The HUD localisation

The HUD overlay is partially localised. The score is universal. The player names render in the local language's transliteration where available. The timestamp uses 24-hour ISO format. The "REPLAY" banner is in English everywhere. The "GOAL" celebration text is in English everywhere. None of these have been audited by native speakers. A pass on the HUD strings, per language, is part of the translation work.

## The opportunity

This is a translation project with a real audience and a real revenue model. The English commentary already drives engagement on the AR-FR replay. Every line you fix in your language ships into the next match the renderer plays, which is every match Tournamental plays from now on.

### The big picture

The localisation fidelity ladder, from where we are to "broadcaster-grade":

- **Level 0**, machine-translation defaults. Ten languages shipped. We are here.
- **Level 1**, native-speaker-reviewed. Each of the ten languages has been audited by a native speaker who has fixed the registry and the cliche placement. Two to three months of focused work.
- **Level 2**, broadcaster-grade. Each language sounds like a real broadcast in the relevant country, with regional variants, per-name pronunciation, and localised crowd loops. Six to nine months.
- **Level 3**, multi-broadcaster optionality. The viewer picks "Mexican broadcast voice" or "Argentine broadcast voice" within the Spanish family, or "Egyptian broadcast voice" within Arabic. Twelve to eighteen months.

We need Level 1 across the existing ten languages and three new languages (Bengali, Korean, Hindi) before the World Cup kicks off. Level 2 across the major language families during the World Cup. Level 3 by the second tournament cycle.

### A category overview

Translation contributions cluster into four buckets:

1. **Script translation.** Reviewing or rewriting the per-language Markdown files for accuracy, registry, and broadcast feel.
2. **Glossary work.** Authoring per-language terminology dictionaries that feed back into the translation pipeline.
3. **TTS pronunciation.** Authoring SSML per-name pronunciation hints, plus per-event prosody hints.
4. **Audio mix and crowd.** Localised crowd loops, per-region ambience, the mix between commentary and crowd.

Each bucket is mostly independent of the others. A translator can stay in bucket 1 indefinitely without touching the others; an audio engineer can stay in bucket 4 without writing a single line of translation. Pick the bucket you have the most experience in.

### A note on tooling

The translation work is text-only at the script level: the per-language Markdown files are edited in any text editor. For glossary work, the recommended tool is a simple structured-text format (CSV or JSON); the project does not require a specific glossary tool like [SDL Trados](https://www.trados.com/) or [memoQ](https://www.memoq.com/). For TTS pronunciation work, the SSML standard is at [W3C](https://www.w3.org/TR/speech-synthesis11/) and the implementation reference is the [ElevenLabs SSML documentation](https://elevenlabs.io/docs/api-reference/text-to-speech).

For audio work, the recommended tools are [Audacity](https://www.audacityteam.org/) (free, cross-platform, good enough for most crowd-loop editing) or [Logic Pro / Pro Tools / Ableton](https://www.apple.com/logic-pro/) for professional work. The exported WAV files go in `apps/web/public/sound/crowd/<region>/`.

### What a translator could ship in one day

- A pass-by-pass review of one language file. Open `data/commentary/wc2022-final-arg-fra/<your-language>.md`. Read the script. Fix the parts that sound wrong. Submit a PR with your changes and a short session note explaining the editorial choices (because the next translator working on the same language will read it). One day is enough for the most egregious fixes; the next contributor refines.
- A registry shift on one language. The Portuguese version, as shipped, is Continental. The fix is to clone `pt.md` to `pt-BR.md`, adjust the registry, and update the `manifest.json` to expose both. One day of work, and the Brazilian audience gets the right voice.
- A per-name SSML pronunciation pass for the player roster. The squad list is in [`apps/statsbomb-replay/data/wc2022-final-players.csv`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/apps/statsbomb-replay/data/wc2022-final-players.csv). For each of the twenty-two starters, you write the correct phonetic transcription in your language and the TTS pipeline picks it up. The [SSML spec](https://www.w3.org/TR/speech-synthesis11/) covers the `<phoneme>` element.

### What a translator could ship in one week

- A full re-authoring of one language version, line by line, in the voice of a real broadcaster from your country. Use the English master as the structural reference (the timing and the event-coverage rhythm) but write the prose in the way the local broadcasters do. The Brazilian version, for instance, should sound like [Galvão Bueno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galv%C3%A3o_Bueno) at the goal moments. The Argentine version should sound like [Pablo Giralt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pablo_Giralt) or [Sebastián Vignolo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_Vignolo). One week per language, and the audio track in your country becomes the reason fans come to Tournamental for the replay instead of YouTube.
- A new language. Bengali, Korean, Swahili, Hindi, Urdu, whichever you speak natively. The pipeline is in [docs/31-live-commentary-overlay.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/31-live-commentary-overlay.md); you author the language file, add the entry to `manifest.json`, and ship.
- A **fan-mode commentary variant** for an existing language. The current script is "neutral broadcaster". The fan-mode variant uses local slang, the home team's nickname, and the rhetorical patterns of supporter-driven commentary (the kind you hear on amateur YouTube channels in your country). One week of work and we ship "watch the AR-FR final in Argentine fan-mode Spanish, with the actual chants from Buenos Aires layered into the crowd track".

### What a translator could ship in one month

- The full commentary pack for the **2026 World Cup matches** as live data arrives. The English master script is being authored as live commentary for every match (the script is event-template-driven and assembles in real time). Your job is the per-language counterpart. One month per language and you are the lead translator for that language for the whole tournament cycle.
- A **regional dialect pack** for one language family. Arabic with Modern Standard plus Egyptian plus Gulf plus Levantine. Spanish with Latin American plus Argentine plus Iberian plus Mexican. Portuguese with Continental plus Brazilian. The pipeline supports per-region voice mapping in `manifest.json` and the renderer reads the user's language preference plus the user's geographic hint to pick the right variant.
- A **terminology dictionary** for your language. The football phrasebook in your country is not a one-to-one mapping with English. The right Italian word for a "long ball over the top" is contextual, the right Russian word for a "tap-in" varies by era, the right Japanese word for a "screamer" is two characters that change meaning if you stress the first syllable wrong. One month of glossary work feeds back into every script the pipeline ever generates.
- A **per-region audio mix** for one language family. Author per-region crowd loops, per-region cheer samples, per-region whistle-cadence samples. The integration is in `apps/web/lib/sound/`. One month for a fully-realised regional audio identity.
- An **in-renderer subtitle layer** in your language. The renderer ships with audio commentary but no subtitle option. A captions track that renders the commentary as on-screen text, in your language, with timing pulled from the manifest. One month of careful work and the renderer becomes accessible to viewers who cannot use audio (hard of hearing, working in a noisy environment, watching on mute).

### The licensing on translation work

The licensing on the commentary scripts is the same as the rest of the docs: **CC-BY-4.0**, attribution required. This means your translation work, once it merges into upstream, is reusable by anyone who wants to build a multilingual football-commentary product. You ship into Tournamental and you also ship into the public corpus.

If you contribute translation work, you retain copyright. The project gets a broad licence to use, redistribute, and modify. The DCO sign-off on the commit captures this. If you prefer a different licence (some translator collectives prefer GPL-style copyleft to ensure their work cannot be embedded in proprietary products), the discussion is open; the default is CC-BY-4.0 and most translators take the default.

### Why translation contributions compound

A line of commentary that ships into the script ships into every replay the renderer ever plays in your language. The marginal cost of each replay is zero. The value compounds across every viewer, forever. A single great goal-call in Brazilian Portuguese, written once, plays for the next thousand goals in the same language. The Drips attribution at the per-line level captures this: your per-line contribution stays in the score sheet for the full decay schedule.

This is also why the council reviews translation PRs slightly more carefully than code PRs. A bad line of code can be reverted in the next PR. A bad line of commentary that ships in audio is harder to retract because users have heard it. The review pass includes a native-speaker review, a registry review, and a broadcast-feel review before merge.

### A worked example of the script structure

Here is a small sample of the English script alongside the Portuguese, Spanish, and French translations as they ship today:

```
## minute 23:18, period 1
[crowd: rising murmur]
LEAD (en): Messi steps up to the spot.
COLOUR (en): He has scored four from four in this tournament from twelve yards.

LEAD (pt): Messi prepara-se para o pénalti.
COLOUR (pt): Marcou quatro em quatro neste torneio dos onze metros.

LEAD (es): Messi se prepara para el penal.
COLOUR (es): Lleva cuatro de cuatro en este torneo desde los once metros.

LEAD (fr): Messi s'avance pour le penalty.
COLOUR (fr): Il a converti quatre tentatives sur quatre dans ce tournoi depuis les onze mètres.
```

The Portuguese is Continental Portuguese ("pénalti" with the accent, "onze metros" for the distance). A Brazilian Portuguese speaker reading this would feel something is off; the right registry would use "pênalti" without the accent and "onze metros" or "marca de pênalti" depending on the broadcast tradition. The Spanish is neutral Latin American; an Argentine speaker would prefer "el penal" with the "che" preamble that the LLM stripped. The French is fine, modulo the player-name pronunciation issue.

This is the kind of pass-by-pass review a translator could ship in a day.

## How Drips pays you

Translation contributors get the same revenue-share programme as code and design contributors. The whole system is documented in [docs/19-open-source-and-contributor-revenue.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/19-open-source-and-contributor-revenue.md). The short version, with the translation-specific bits called out:

### The Drips Network primer

Tournamental's revenue routes through [Drips Network](https://www.drips.network/) to a list of contributor wallets. Drips is the same protocol that the [Ethereum Foundation](https://ethereum.foundation/), [Radworks](https://radworks.org/), the [Filecoin Foundation](https://fil.org/), [ENS](https://ens.domains/), [UNICEF](https://www.unicef.org/) and [Scroll](https://scroll.io/) use to stream funding to open-source contributors. It is on-chain. It is transparent. You can read the contract, fork it, propose a better split formula, or audit the multisig that controls the foundation's treasury wallet.

### The treasury policy

The Tournamental Foundation deposits a fixed percentage of net affiliate revenue and other monetization lanes (the replay-stream Pro subscription, the B2B white-label revenue, the affiliate clicks to sportsbooks and Polymarket where legal) into a Drips treasury wallet, paid in USDC. The current split is **thirty percent to contributors, fifty percent to the operating reserve (capped at twelve months), ten percent to public goods, ten percent to strategic reserve**.

Once the operating reserve cap is full, more flows to contributors. The split rebalances annually based on actual revenue.

### The scoring formula, for translation work

For commentary work specifically, the scoring formula in doc 19 treats translation as a category with the following weights:

- **Per-language flat bounty** on a fully-authored language script (acceptance criteria: all 2,972 lines reviewed, manifest updated, voice mapping configured, native-speaker sign-off from one other contributor).
- **Per-line weight** on incremental fixes inside an existing language file. Capped per file to discourage rephrasing-farming.
- **Reviews of other translators' PRs** earn at twenty percent of the original PR score. A native speaker who reviews a junior translator's work gets a slice.
- **Major terminology contributions** (the glossary, the dialect-pack RFC, the fan-mode variant authoring) earn the same per-feature bounty as a significant code feature.

### The revenue flow

Every paid affiliate click and every paid replay-stream view that traces back to a Tournamental product surface in your language routes a slice through Drips to your address. If a Brazilian fan upgrades to the Pro replay-stream subscription to watch the World Cup in Brazilian Portuguese, your work on the Brazilian language file is part of the credit chain for that subscription. The attribution is rule-based and the rules are in doc 19, public and dispute-able.

### Decay and dilution

Decay is the same five-quarter schedule: a contribution made today is worth one hundred percent next quarter, eighty the quarter after, then sixty, forty, twenty, zero. Forces ongoing contribution. Translation, in particular, is a category where contributions age fast (player names change, registry changes, new clubs join leagues), so the decay schedule maps well to the work.

### A worked example of the payout flow

Imagine you ship the Brazilian Portuguese variant in your first PR. The PR is a fully-authored language file plus the manifest update, scored as a per-language flat bounty plus the per-line credit on the new file. The total score is 1,500 points.

If your score is 2.3 percent of the total contributor score for Q1 2026, and the Drip List receives 30,000 USDC for the quarter (notional; actual depends on platform revenue, which we publish openly), your share is 690 USDC, streamed continuously over the quarter into your wallet.

In Q2, your Q1 contribution decays to 80 percent. If you also ship the per-name SSML pronunciation pass in Q2, your Q2 contribution adds on top. The quarterly distribution adjusts.

If you stop contributing, your stream tapers across five quarters and ends. If the project shuts down, the stream ends sooner.

### The wallet integration

The wallet integration is the same as the rest of the contributor programme. You register your GitHub username with an Ethereum address. Sanctions screening runs at payout time. The quarterly score sheet is public in `payouts/`. Multisig-controlled, transparent, fork-able.

If your translation work involves recording voice samples (for a future TTS voice trained on a real broadcaster from your country, which is on the roadmap), the recording usage rights are negotiated separately and the Drips share is a per-minute royalty on top of the contributor stream. We have not shipped this yet but the design is in doc 19.

### Comparable projects

If you want a sanity-check on whether "open source plus revenue share via Drips for translation" is a credible model, the precedents are:

- **[Mozilla Foundation](https://foundation.mozilla.org/)**, where the Firefox localisation project pays contributors via grants and the [Common Voice](https://commonvoice.mozilla.org/) project crowdsources translated voice samples under CC-0.
- **[LibreOffice](https://www.libreoffice.org/)**, whose translation contributors are paid via TDF grants for major language packs.
- **[Crowdin](https://crowdin.com/)**, which is the dominant commercial platform for translation but does not have a revenue-share model.
- **[Tatoeba](https://tatoeba.org/)**, a CC-BY translated-sentence corpus that operates as a non-profit with grant funding.

Tournamental's revenue-share model for translation is closer to the Mozilla / Common Voice path: the work feeds back into a CC-BY corpus, and the financial path is on-chain rather than grant-based.

### Opting out is fine

You do not have to opt in. The licence is CC-BY-4.0 either way. Some translators prefer not to receive payments for tax, personal, or philosophical reasons, and that is fine.

## How to start

1. Clone the repo: `git clone https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental`.
2. Run the AR-FR demo to hear the current commentary in your language. The README has the exact commands; at `--time-scale=10` the whole final plays in fifteen minutes with audio. Open `localhost:3000/match/fifa-wc-2022-final-arg-fra-2022-12-18?lang=<your-language>` (e.g. `?lang=pt` for Portuguese).
3. Open `data/commentary/wc2022-final-arg-fra/<your-language>.md`. Read it side-by-side with `en.md`. Make notes. The Markdown structure is `## minute MM:SS` headings with the commentary text and the optional `[crowd: cheer]` or `[colour: <line>]` inline tags.
4. Read [docs/31-live-commentary-overlay.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/31-live-commentary-overlay.md) for the pipeline architecture and the manifest spec. Read [CONTRIBUTING.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/CONTRIBUTING.md) for the PR template and the DCO sign-off rule.
5. Open a PR with your changes. Include a session note at `sessions/<date>_translator-<your-name>_<language>.md` explaining your editorial choices. The session-note discipline is in [AGENT-PROMPTS.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/AGENT-PROMPTS.md).

### The session-note discipline for translators

### A note on what "broadcaster-grade" actually means

The phrase "broadcaster-grade" is doing work in this post. To unpack it: I mean the commentary track should sound like something a viewer in your country would believe came out of a local broadcaster's booth. Not perfect. Not professional-grade. Believable.

The key markers a native speaker checks:

- **Goal-call cadence**. The vowel-stretch, the volume swell, the pause before the colour-commentator picks up.
- **Player-name pronunciation**. Stress on the right syllable, the right vowel quality, the right consonant articulation.
- **Set-piece terminology**. The word for "free kick", "penalty", "corner", "throw-in" in the right register for the audience.
- **Tactical idiom**. The word for "long ball over the top", "tap-in", "screamer", "thunderbolt". Each has a regional variation.
- **Emotional cadence on tense moments**. The build-up to a Messi free kick is a different rhetorical pattern than the build-up to an Mbappé penalty.
- **Colour-commentator pacing**. The lead and colour commentators have different rhetorical roles in every tradition.

If your translation gets all six right, the commentary sounds like a real broadcast. If it gets four out of six right, it sounds passable but slightly off. If it gets two right, it sounds like a translated PDF.

The script in the pack today is at "passable" for the four reviewed languages and "translated PDF" for the five unreviewed ones. Our goal is "broadcaster-grade" across the board, and that requires native speakers to do the work.

### A specific rant about broadcast registry

Let me get specific about one thing because it matters for the work. Football commentary has a specific rhetorical structure that is different from general spoken language in every country I have ever paid attention to. The structure is, roughly:

- **Build.** A few seconds of low-energy descriptive language. "Argentina rebuilding from the back. Cuti Romero to Otamendi."
- **Tension.** A few seconds of rising language. "Lo Celso pushes up. Messi drops deep."
- **Climax.** The goal call or the near-miss. High energy. Vowel-stretch on the key word.
- **Reflection.** The colour commentator picks up. Lower energy. Tactical analysis. Context.

In English, this structure is well-developed and most commentators follow it. In other languages, the structure exists but the rhythm differs. Brazilian Portuguese tends to compress the build and stretch the climax. Argentine Spanish stretches both the build and the climax. Japanese tends to keep the build flat and inject all the energy into the climax via prosody rather than word choice. Arabic broadcast convention varies wildly by country.

The current scripts in our pack mostly follow the English rhythm structure because the LLM translated from the English. A native-speaker translator's job is to **re-rhythm** the script to match the local broadcast convention. This is not a sentence-level translation problem; it is a paragraph-level rhythm problem. The translation review PR is therefore not just "fix the words" but "fix the cadence".

If you have ever sat through five minutes of broadcast football in your country, you know the cadence. The script in the pack does not have it. Your job is to put it back.

### The TTS rendering pipeline, in detail

The TTS rendering pipeline takes the per-language Markdown script plus the manifest and produces an audio file plus a per-line timing track. The pipeline lives at `apps/web/lib/commentary/tts.ts` and calls the ElevenLabs API.

The flow per language:

1. Parse the Markdown into utterances. Each `## minute X:Y, period N` heading defines an utterance group.
2. Resolve the voice mapping for the language from `manifest.json`.
3. For each utterance, build the SSML payload. Apply per-name pronunciation overrides from the pronunciations file.
4. POST to ElevenLabs `/v1/text-to-speech/<voice_id>` with the SSML.
5. Receive the MP3 stream. Concatenate by minute.
6. Write the output to `apps/web/public/audio/commentary/wc2022-final-arg-fra/<lang>.mp3`.
7. Write the per-line timing track to `apps/web/public/audio/commentary/wc2022-final-arg-fra/<lang>.timing.json`.

The timing track is what the renderer uses to sync the audio cues to the match events. The pipeline takes about four minutes per language for the full 200-minute match (mostly synthesis time at the ElevenLabs API).

If you ship a translation PR, the pipeline runs as part of the CI step. The reviewer agent automatically renders the new audio and attaches the MP3 to the PR comments so the reviewer can listen to the change.

### A specific issue I have not solved: the live-feed lag

In the live-commentary path, there is an unsolved problem with event-to-audio latency. The StatsBomb-style live feed delivers events with a typical 5-to-10-second delay from the actual on-pitch moment. The TTS render adds another 250-to-500 milliseconds. The total lag means the audio commentary lags the visual by 5-to-10 seconds, which feels disorienting if the viewer is also watching a live TV broadcast on a second screen.

The fix is one of:

- **Show the visual at the same lag as the audio**, so the renderer's clock matches the audio clock and the user experiences a coherent 5-to-10-second delay.
- **Synthesise the commentary speculatively**, based on the predicted next event, and correct if the prediction is wrong. The speculative path is in research but not shipped.
- **Pre-render generic event commentary** for the common event types and trigger them based on the predicted event, with corrections if needed.

I do not know which is the right answer. A translator with a strong opinion on live-commentary latency would be a useful voice in this discussion.

### Frequently asked questions

**Do I need to be a professional translator?**

No. Native fluency in the target language plus a good ear for sports broadcast is enough. Many of the best translation contributors at projects like [Mozilla's Common Voice](https://commonvoice.mozilla.org/) are amateurs with strong cultural grounding.

**What if I am not a sports fan but I am multilingual?**

Football commentary specifically requires sports literacy. If you do not know the difference between a cross and a through-ball, the cliché slots will land wrong. If you are willing to learn the football vocabulary plus the broadcast cadence, that is fine; otherwise, the audio HUD strings and the user-interface localisation (separate work, in `apps/web/i18n/`) is a better fit.

**Can I work with a co-translator?**

Yes. Multi-author PRs are supported via the `Co-authored-by` trailer in the commit message. Both authors are credited on the score sheet. Many of the best translation pairs are a registry expert plus a broadcast-cadence specialist working together.

**What if my dialect is not in the existing pack?**

Add it. Author a new variant. The pipeline supports any number of regional variants per language family. The manifest is the registry.

**What if I record voice samples for a future TTS voice trained on a broadcaster?**

That work is on the roadmap and the design is in doc 19. The voice samples become a separate IP asset with a per-minute royalty on top of the Drips stream. We have not shipped this yet; if you want to be in the first cohort, send an email.

**Can I rebrand the commentary in a fork?**

The scripts are CC-BY-4.0. You can fork them, modify them, and use them in your own project as long as you attribute. The brand assets (Tournamental name, wordmark) are not open and you cannot use them.

### What the project does not need from a translator

To save you time, the things that are **not** in scope for translation contributions:

- **Wholesale rewrites of the English master**. The master is owned by Tim and the editorial council. Changes require an RFC.
- **Adding new commentary tracks that are not synchronised to the manifest**. The manifest is the source of truth for timing; tracks that do not sync are not playable.
- **TTS voice changes that require commercial licence**. We use the ElevenLabs catalogue plus a small set of free voices. Custom-trained voices are a separate roadmap item with their own commercial path.
- **Subtitles in formats other than the manifest-driven timing track**. The renderer's subtitle layer reads from the timing track; SRT or VTT exports are derivable from the timing track but not the source of truth.
- **Localisation of the marketing site**. The marketing site is currently English-only and the localisation is a separate, larger discussion involving the Astro content-collection plumbing.

### One specific moment I want to flag

Inside the AR-FR final, the most-replayed moment is the Mbappé hat-trick (his goal at 80 minutes from the penalty, his goal at 81 minutes from open play, and his penalty at 118 minutes in extra time). The three goal calls in your language are the most-listened-to lines in the whole script. I do not know how each of those three goal calls sounds in your country. The Brazilian commentary tradition for a Mbappé goal probably differs from the Argentine tradition. The Japanese tradition probably differs from the Arabic. Your job is to put the right one in.

The live path differs from the replay path in a few ways:

- **Lower latency**. The TTS render needs to happen within 250 milliseconds of the event arriving on the wire. ElevenLabs WebSocket streaming is the path; the integration is at `apps/web/lib/commentary/live.ts`.
- **Lower richness**. The live data has fewer fields per event than the StatsBomb post-match data. The script generation needs to handle missing data gracefully.
- **Per-language streaming**. Each viewer's session subscribes to a single language stream; the server-side render scales per-language.
- **Fallback to text**. When the TTS fails (rate limits, voice unavailable, network issue), the renderer falls back to on-screen text in the viewer's language.

For translators, the live path means the per-language work is not a one-time deliverable. The 2026 World Cup runs for a month with multiple matches per day, and the per-language commentary is generated live for each match. Translators who have contributed to the existing pack will have their work referenced by the live-script generator, but the live cadence and the per-event details require ongoing per-match passes during the tournament.

If you commit to a language for the World Cup window, you become the de-facto lead translator for that language for the month, with the existing pack as the foundation and the live matches as the work. This is one of the highest-impact-per-hour contribution windows on the project.
