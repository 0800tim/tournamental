## Who this article is for

The producer on the show. The head of digital. The on-air graphics lead. The OB-truck engineer. Anyone who has to make the prize draw actually appear on screen during a live broadcast and who needs the answer to be "yes that drops in cleanly" rather than "we'll need to build a bespoke widget."

The short version: Tournamental ships **four integration surfaces** that cover essentially every production team's existing tooling. Pick the one that matches your truck.

## Surface 1: OBS / vMix HTML overlays

For shows produced through OBS or vMix (most podcasts, most digital-first shows, smaller cable productions, web-streaming events), our overlay is a single URL pointing to an HTML page sized to your canvas. Drop it in as a **Browser Source** in OBS or a **Web Browser** input in vMix.

The overlay is 1920×1080 with full alpha, listens to a WebSocket from our stream service, and re-renders any of four standard layouts:

- **Live leaderboard, top 10.** Names, current scores, movement arrows.
- **Big-prize counter.** "1.2M entries · £1M still in play · 14 brackets still perfect."
- **Last-pick spotlight.** "Stuart from Auckland just picked Brazil to win it all."
- **Settlement segment.** Full-screen winner reveal with confetti and the share-card.

Configuration is via URL query parameters: `?layout=leaderboard&theme=skysports&logo=/cdn/skysports.svg&refresh=15`. Your graphics lead can rehearse against test data the day they get the URL. No build pipeline. No deploy. No node_modules.

The overlay theme can be a built-in (six options shipped, blue / red / gold / black / white / minimal) or a custom theme delivered to you as a single CSS file. Custom themes are part of the 48-hour spin-up.

## Surface 2: vMix data sources + Newtek graphics

For productions with serious broadcast graphics, vMix Data Sources, Newtek LiveText, ChyronHego, Vizrt, ross graphics, every modern broadcast graphics package can pull from a JSON or XML feed at a configurable poll interval.

We expose a **JSON feed** at `https://api.tournamental.com/v1/broadcast/<your-slug>/leaderboard.json` (and `/program.json`, `/winners.json`, `/perfect-brackets-remaining.json`). Sample response:

```json
{
  "updatedAt": "2026-06-23T14:32:08Z",
  "leaderboard": [
    { "rank": 1, "handle": "stuie_picks", "city": "Auckland", "score": 142, "delta": 0 },
    { "rank": 2, "handle": "nzfooty",     "city": "Wellington", "score": 138, "delta": -1 },
    { "rank": 3, "handle": "talksport_pundit", "city": "Manchester", "score": 135, "delta": +2 }
  ],
  "totals": {
    "entries": 1247902,
    "perfectBracketsRemaining": 14,
    "currentPrizePool": 1000000,
    "currentPrizeCurrency": "GBP"
  }
}
```

Field-level filtering via query parameters (e.g. `?city=Auckland`, `?country=NZ`) for regional leaderboards. Caching is short on our side (60-second edge TTL), so polling at 15-30 seconds is well within the rate limit.

For productions that prefer XML or CSV, the same endpoints accept `Accept: text/xml` or `Accept: text/csv`. The CSV format is friendly to spreadsheet-based graphics workflows that still exist in some shops.

## Surface 3: Live-stream WebSocket for low-latency tickers

For shows that want the leaderboard to **animate in real time** (rather than polling), we have a WebSocket endpoint that pushes updates as they happen:

```
wss://stream.tournamental.com/broadcast/<your-slug>
```

Messages are JSON, one per ws.send. The shape is the same primary-stream protocol our renderer consumes (see [docs/58-data-producers.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/58-data-producers.md)), with a couple of broadcast-specific event types:

- `broadcast.leaderboard.update` (rank changes for the top-100 leaderboard).
- `broadcast.prize.update` (perfect-brackets-remaining counter, prize-pool changes).
- `broadcast.winner.locked` (when a major prize is awarded, a single winner-card payload).

Web-based broadcast graphics frameworks (Singular.live, Caspar, Tovera, NodeCG) plug into this directly. For OBS / vMix users, the HTML overlays in Surface 1 are subscribed to this WebSocket under the hood, you get the same animation behaviour without touching the protocol.

## Surface 4: On-air QR codes + branded landing

The most common production request: a QR code on screen that drops viewers into the contest entry flow in two taps.

We deliver:

- **A 1024×1024 SVG QR code** with your network logo embedded in the centre, scannable from a couch at typical TV viewing distance.
- **A branded landing page** at `<network>.tournamental.com` or your own domain via CNAME. Your colours, your sponsor placement, your call-to-action.
- **A deep-link contract**: scanning during the World Cup drops the viewer into the bracket entry flow with your network's source-attribution baked in. Scanning during a regional segment can drop them into a city-specific leaderboard.

The QR code is regenerable per-segment if you want different landings for different show blocks. Most networks pick one master landing for the tournament and use show-segment URLs for sub-prizes.

## Standard production timeline

For a one-week pre-show rehearsal:

- **Mon**: Discovery call. Confirm format, prize, branding, integration surfaces, talent involvement.
- **Tue**: Branded landing live on staging. Graphics lead gets overlay URL + JSON feed URL + WebSocket URL.
- **Wed**: Graphics rehearsal against test data. We sit on a Zoom for the first hour.
- **Thu**: Talent walkthrough. Host runs the segment to camera with real overlay. We make notes for tweaks.
- **Fri**: Final review. Greenlight if all four surfaces render clean on the production truck.
- **Sat**: Promo segment airs on a regular show ("watch this space, we're announcing something huge on Monday").
- **Mon (week 2)**: Contest live.

For productions with longer lead times (national TV networks, season-long contests) the timeline stretches to give legal and procurement breathing room. The technology is rarely the bottleneck.

To make the call efficient:

## Talent-friendly: what the host actually sees

A common production worry: "is this going to be technical-feeling on air?" No. The host segment is a leaderboard, a name, a city, and a number. We supply a host-card template:

> "Tonight's bracket champion is **Stuart from Auckland**, currently sitting on **142 points**, eight matches ahead of second place. Stuart, if you stay on top through the final, you're walking away with **£1,000,000**. Twitter is going wild. Let's go to the verifier..."

The verifier shot (Surface 4) is the credibility beat. The leaderboard is the human-interest beat. The host doesn't have to say the word "blockchain" once if they don't want to. The audience just sees their fellow viewers winning real money on a verifiable scoreboard.

For hosts who *want* to lean into the verification angle (the BBC, the FT, anyone with a "this is how the technology works" audience), we supply a 30-second host-script explainer plus a graphic that walks through the Merkle proof in three steps. Used sparingly, it lands like a magic-trick reveal.

## What it costs

Surfaces 1-4 are **free to integrate** under the Tournamental open partner model for any network running a free-to-enter prediction game. We charge an integration fee only when:

- We're brokering insurance for a big-prize format (we take a small commission from the underwriter).
- We're providing dedicated overlay-design hours beyond the standard templates (custom Singular.live compositions, bespoke OBS scenes).
- We're operating high-traffic infrastructure (>5M entries in a single contest needs dedicated capacity).

For a typical regional radio or mid-tier digital show, the integration is genuinely free. We make our money on the headline contests; we want the long tail of broadcasters running their own smaller draws because that's where the platform gets its credibility.
