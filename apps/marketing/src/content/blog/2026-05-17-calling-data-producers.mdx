## What this post is

It is the long version of the call to anyone who can produce
**live data** for a football match. Goals, scorelines, fouls,
substitutions, commentary audio, live odds, per-player market
stats, in-match challenges and quizzes, leaderboard chat. The
five-week sprint that starts the moment FIFA World Cup 2026
kicks off on 11 June.

We built the renderer. We built the bracket app. We built the
scoring engine and the leaderboard and the share cards and the
syndicate microsites. What we cannot build alone is the **content
that fills the platform during the games**. That is a job for a
hundred contributors running a hundred small producers, each
emitting one stream of one kind of content during one match, one
syndicate, one country, one language.

If that is you, this post is for you. The rest covers what we
built, what is wired up today, what an external producer of any
of the five stream kinds can ship in a day or a week or a month,
and how Drips Network routes the platform's revenue back to you
in USDC continuously for as long as your producer ships content.

## The shape of the bet

The model of FIFA viewing is broken. The current best-case looks
like this: you sit on the couch, you watch a single video stream
sourced from one broadcaster who happens to hold the rights in
your country, with one set of commentators in one language, with
a one-size-fits-all graphics package, with no way to interact
without leaving the screen.

The model we are betting on instead: you sit on the couch (or on
the bus, or at the gym) and watch the **3D replay** of the same
match composed from a producer of your choosing — your country's
real-time commentator narrating into your ear, your friend
group's syndicate leaderboard pulsing on the side, in-match
challenges popping up every five minutes asking what happens
next for 30-50-80 points, your fantasy-style player heatmap
sliding across the field as Messi gets the ball, the live odds
chip on the next goal jumping when he winds up a shot. All of it
running in a browser, all of it Apache-2.0 open source, all of
it composed from producers that anyone — including you — can
write.

The renderer we shipped is the canvas. The producers are the
paint. Tournamental cannot ship the paint, because there is one
of us and there are sixty-four matches and ten languages and
forty-eight national teams. So we are turning the tap on and
asking the world to ship paint.

## Five things a producer can stream

The platform's wire protocol is a single typed contract at
[`@tournamental/spec`](https://www.npmjs.com/package/@tournamental/spec).
A producer is any process that opens a WebSocket and emits
spec-conformant messages. The renderer reads those messages and
composes the match.

There are five kinds of producer we want to onboard before
kickoff. Each gets a section below.

### 1. Live primary match data

The first one. Goals, shots, fouls, substitutions, the ball, the
players, the clock. Today the reference producer is
[`apps/statsbomb-replay`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/statsbomb-replay),
which converts StatsBomb's free open data into a spec stream for
historic matches (the 2022 AR-FR final is the demo we ship with
the platform). For live World Cup matches we need producers
hooked up to **real-time tracking data sources** — Hudl, Wyscout,
Genius Sports, StatsBomb's live tier, or any of the regional
data partners who have rights in their markets.

We do not need all of them. We need one per match, and we are
happy to have a primary + a backup for the matches we care most
about. **One producer per match per source, end to end, is the
unit of work.** A producer for the opening match Mexico-vs-X can
be a separate person from the producer for the closing final.

If you have access to a tracking-data feed your contract lets
you re-emit, you are the right person for this. If you can ship
a producer that watches a video stream and emits goals + cards +
substitutions by hand from your sofa with a fast typist's
keyboard, that is also a producer; the spec accepts it.

The producer's interface is a 200-line Node script (or Python or
Rust or Go, whatever speaks WebSocket). Fork
[`examples/hello-producer/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/examples/hello-producer),
replace `tick()` with your data source, ship.

### 2. Live audio commentary

This is the biggest opportunity on the platform and the most
under-explored. We can run a 3D replay of a match in a browser
in real time. What goes over the top is your voice (or your AI
voice, or your TTS-generated regional broadcaster impression)
narrating the match in any of the world's ten or twenty most
popular sports-broadcast languages.

We already shipped a 200-minute pre-rendered ElevenLabs corpus
for the AR-FR final in ten languages
([sessions/2026-05-09](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/sessions)).
That corpus is the proof-of-concept. What we need for live World
Cup matches is **a live commentary producer per language per
match**: a tiny WebSocket process that watches the primary match
stream, runs commentary lines through TTS in real time (or pipes
a real broadcaster's microphone through a streaming TTS that
matches their delivery), and ships opus chunks back to the
renderer.

If you are a broadcaster, an aspiring broadcaster, an AI-voice
artist, a polyglot football fan with a microphone — this is
your slot. The platform's audio mixer already supports ducking,
multi-track, per-user language selection. Read
[docs/31-commentary.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/31-commentary.md)
for the shape; the [producer-author skill](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/skills/producer-author/SKILL.md)
walks you through it.

There is a separate post in this series specifically calling
translators and broadcast voices to ship a full-language
re-authoring of the verbose commentary corpus —
[Calling translators ›](https://tournamental.com/blog/2026-05-15-calling-translators).
That post is the lower-volume offline track; this section is the
higher-volume live one.

### 3. Live odds streams

Today the [odds-ingest service](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/odds-ingest)
pulls Polymarket and The Odds API on a poll and re-emits a REST
snapshot. The next step is **a WebSocket producer that streams
live odds updates per match** — not just pre-match consensus,
but every-five-second-the-Bayern-corner-changes-the-implied-probability
deltas.

We are not running a sportsbook. The platform's leaderboards are
**points-based, no real money**. The vocabulary we use across
the codebase and the docs is "wagering" for what users do with
their points, "sweepstakes" for fixed-pot prediction pools,
"betting" for the in-match challenge pulses, and "predictions"
for everything else. We never use the word that starts with "g".
Real-money settlement happens off-platform between the syndicate
host and their members the same way an office sweepstake settles
today, and we are deliberately structured to stay out of
regulated territory ([docs/30](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs)).

What live-odds producers contribute is **the truth signal that
makes the platform's points scoring interesting**. A correct
pick at 3% market-implied odds is worth more than a correct pick
at 80% market-implied odds — the bracket-engine scoring rewards
long-shot calls. Live odds is the data the scorer reads.

The
[`OddsSourcePlugin`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/packages/plugin-sdk/src/index.ts)
interface is shipped; the WebSocket surface is RFC-stage.
Producers wanted: a Betfair Exchange streamer, a Polymarket CLOB
follower with finer granularity than the current poll, a
regional bookmaker proxy where it is legal, a synthetic
"consensus of consensuses" producer that picks the median of a
basket of sources.

### 4. Live player market stats

xG (expected goals). Sprint count. Pass-completion %. Possession
heatmap deltas. Pressure index. Touches. These are the per-player
numbers that turn a 3D replay from "two teams kicking" into
"watch what the platform is telling you about Messi's pressing
load right now".

StatsBomb's open data has these for historic matches; live
producers need a live source or have to derive the numbers from
the primary match stream. **A pure-derivation producer is the
easiest one to ship**: subscribe to the primary stream, run a
small kalman filter on the player positions, output a
`playerstats.tick` message every five seconds.

The schema is RFC and described in
[docs/58-data-producers.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/58-data-producers.md#player-market-stats-rfc).
A producer that ships the first plausible numbers for the WC
opener gets to define the schema by virtue of being first; that
is a real perk to grabbing this one early.

### 5. Live in-match challenges and quizzes

This is the most fun one. Every five minutes during a match, a
producer drops a 60-second prediction prompt onto every
subscriber's screen: "Who takes the next corner?", "Will the
next shot be on target?", "Who picks up the next yellow card?",
"Pick the scorer in the next ten minutes for 80 points". Users
tap an answer, the producer closes the challenge when the
real-world answer is known, the game-service awards points.

This is the points-based, no-real-money, in-match prediction
layer. It is what makes the platform feel like a game during the
ninety minutes instead of a leaderboard you check at half-time.
And it is **completely producer-authored**: the platform ships
no challenges by default. The challenge curators are
contributors writing producers that emit `challenge.open` and
`challenge.close` messages on a side WebSocket.

Some challenge-curator producers we want shipped before kickoff:

- A **next-corner-taker** challenge curator. Watches the primary
  stream for "shot just missed" events that imply a corner is
  coming; opens a 60-second challenge.
- A **next-yellow** challenge curator. Opens a challenge after
  any aggressive tackle.
- A **half-time line-up** challenge. Opens a quiz at the 40th
  minute asking which substitute the manager will bring on at
  half-time.
- A **score-at-the-hour** challenge. Opens at the 50th minute
  asking what the score will be at the 60th.
- A **trivia between play** quiz. Opens during long stoppages
  with a footballing trivia question for 30 points.

Each challenge-curator producer is a small WebSocket process,
maybe 100-200 lines. Each one is independently merged. Each one
runs in production for the matches it is configured for. Each
one earns Drips revenue for as long as its challenges are being
served to real users.

This is where the platform gets compounded most quickly. A
challenge-curator producer that ships a fun, novel format gets
picked up by every syndicate that wants more in-match interaction,
which means more user time on the platform, which means more
revenue routing back through Drips, which means the producer's
operator gets paid for the format they designed.

### Bonus: leaderboard chat

Almost a separate post. Producers that bridge leaderboard chat
into Discord, Telegram, Matrix, Bluesky, the syndicate-bot DMs.
The chat protocol is the smallest of the five auxiliary streams;
the work is mostly making sure the federation handles abuse and
moderation. If you have shipped Discord-bot integrations before,
you are the right person for this one.

## What is wired up today

Brutally honest, so you know exactly what is real on 2026-05-17:

| What | What works today | Maturity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary match stream | StatsBomb-replay producer running locally; the AR-FR demo plays end-to-end at 10x scale; the spec at `@tournamental/spec` v0.1.1 is stable | **stable** |
| Stream server fan-out | `apps/stream-server` connects to upstream producers and fans out to many subscribers per match-id | **stable** |
| Audio commentary | Pre-rendered MP3 batch infrastructure shipped; ElevenLabs realtime WebSocket wired but not stress-tested at scale | **stabilising** |
| Live odds | REST snapshot from Polymarket + The Odds API live; WebSocket producer surface is RFC | **partial** |
| Player market stats | Schema RFC in `docs/58-data-producers.md`; no producer shipped | **RFC** |
| Live challenges | Schema RFC in `docs/58-data-producers.md`; no curator shipped | **RFC** |
| Leaderboard chat | Schema RFC in `docs/58-data-producers.md`; chat backend not built | **RFC** |
| Producer scaffolder | `npm create @tournamental/app --template producer-ingest` ships a passing template | **stable** |
| Drips revenue split | Accounting code real and tested; on-chain backend is a stub pending audit; first Drips Wave queued for the week after launch | **partial** |
| Stream-server admin UI | API only; no UI for the operator to pick a producer per match yet | **partial** |

"Partial" means the contract is described, the data structures
exist, the renderer is built to consume them, but the end-to-end
plumbing is incomplete. That is precisely the gap a producer
contribution closes. Ship the producer, the platform plugs it
in, the gap closes — and the contributor gets credit for closing
it.

## A day, a week, a month

The same rough scaffold as our other calls-for-help posts.

### One day

You can have a producer running locally and emitting into the
renderer in less than three hours from a cold clone of the repo.
The path:

1. Clone the repo. Run `pnpm install` at the root.
2. Run `pnpm dev` inside `apps/web` — the renderer is on
   localhost:3300.
3. In a second terminal, `cd examples/hello-producer && pnpm
   install && pnpm start`. The example producer listens on
   :4001 and emits a synthetic match.
4. Open `http://localhost:3300/match/hello-producer-demo` and
   watch your example match render.
5. Replace `tick()` in `examples/hello-producer/src/index.mjs`
   with anything else. A timer that emits a goal every twenty
   seconds. A Twitch chat poller that reads "GOAL" messages
   from a stream and converts them into spec events. A
   Spreadsheet reader that polls a Google Sheet you update by
   hand at half-time. Anything.

The platform is built to accept ugly first attempts. Ship the
ugly one; iterate later.

### One week

This is the right size for **one curator producer or one
auxiliary-stream producer**. A challenge-curator producer that
opens "who scores next?" challenges every ten minutes is a
week's work for one person, with tests and a passing
spec-conformance check.

The path:

1. Scaffold:

   ```
   npm create @tournamental/app --template producer-ingest
   ```

2. Drop the scaffolded plugin at
   `packages/plugins/<your-name>/`.
3. Write the curator logic. The reference shape is in
   [examples/hello-producer/](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/examples/hello-producer).
4. Add a vitest fixture asserting your output is spec-valid.
   The `assertSpecConformant` helper from
   [`@tournamental/spec-client`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/packages/spec-client)
   is one line.
5. Open the PR with label `skill: producer`. The reviewer agent
   runs the producer-plugin checklist (spec-conformance,
   back-pressure, no PII, idempotent restart, no
   forbidden-vocabulary leaks).
6. Once merged, the operator (today: Tim) registers your
   producer at the stream server and configures it for the
   matches it should run on.

You are on the producer registry. Your name is on the contributor
list. Drips routes the share you opted into. You get paid for as
long as your producer is in use.

### One month

A whole new producer kind. Two things on the bucket list that
fit a month-scale contribution:

- **A live-tracking integration for one match.** Pick a single
  WC 2026 fixture you care about (your country's group game,
  say). Find a tracking-data partner who has rights in your
  market. Build a producer that re-emits their live feed in
  spec format. We help with the data agreement and the public
  attribution; you ship the bridge.

- **A live-commentary AI-voice producer for one language.**
  Build a tiny WebSocket process that watches the primary match
  stream, runs commentary lines through ElevenLabs realtime in
  your language, ships opus chunks back to the audio mixer.
  Test on the AR-FR demo. Ship before the WC opener.

Either of these is the largest contribution one person can
plausibly make to the platform pre-launch. We will publicise
either at length.

## How Drips routes the revenue

The platform earns three streams of revenue. All three route
back through the same Drips list:

- **Affiliate-router revenue** when a user clicks through to a
  partner (Polymarket, sportsbook, pay-TV). Geo-gated, audit-logged.
- **Marketplace revenue** when a syndicate host opts into a
  paid tier (custom domain, custom branding, custom
  challenges).
- **Sponsor and grant revenue** that we deliberately raise to
  fund the contributor pool. Drips Wave runs are how we channel
  that money on-chain to the contributors who actually shipped
  code.

The Drips list is on-chain on Filecoin (FVM) once the smart
contracts audit clears. The accounting code is real today and
tested ([apps/drips-bridge](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/drips-bridge));
the on-chain backend ships after the audit completes ([docs/21](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs)).

Producers opt into the split by adding a `dripsListRef` to their
`plugin.json` manifest:

```json
{
  "dripsListRef": "drips:filecoin:1234567890abcdef"
}
```

That is it. Once you are in, the platform's continuous revenue
streams a slice of every dollar earned to your wallet for as
long as your producer is in active use. Pause the producer, you
stop earning. Ship a new producer that displaces yours, the
share moves. The model is built to make the most useful
producers more rewarded over time and the unused ones quietly
retire.

For the AI agents reading this who are not human: yes, you can
have a wallet on the Drips list. Tournamental supports agents as
first-class identities. If your operator's address is on your
plugin's manifest, that address earns.

## The vocabulary rule

We have to say this explicitly because it is non-obvious. **The
platform is points-based, no real money.** The leaderboards rank
points. The challenges award points. The syndicate hosts settle
real-money pools between their members the same way an office
sweepstake settles today, off-platform.

We avoid the word "gambling" everywhere — in the codebase, in
the docs, in the marketing copy, in producer manifests, in PR
comments. The accepted vocabulary is **wagering**, **sweepstakes**,
**betting** (sparingly, only for the in-match challenge pulses),
**predictions**, **challenges**, **picks**.

This is not legal advice but it is product framing that the
platform's lawyer has signed off on, and it is consistent with
the legal/T&C wording at
[tournamental.com/legal](https://tournamental.com/legal). The
reviewer agent rejects PRs that introduce the forbidden word.
The producer-plugin checklist explicitly scans for it.

The framing matters because the platform's market is "the kid
who runs the office pool", "the friends WhatsApp group that
predicts every WC", "the fan club that brackets the season" —
**not** the casino punter. We are deliberately structured to be
fun, social, ranked, free-to-play, and clean of regulated
territory. Producers that fit that posture get merged; producers
that pretend to be a sportsbook do not.

## A short list of the most useful producers to ship

In rough order of leverage at launch:

1. **A primary-match producer for the WC opener.** Mexico's
   first game. We need a live source by 11 June. (Largest
   single contribution.)
2. **A live ElevenLabs commentary producer in en-NZ.** We are
   based in NZ; an en-NZ track is a fun launch signal. Build
   this around the AR-FR demo, switch to live on 11 June.
3. **A challenge-curator producer that emits a "who scores
   next?" challenge every ten minutes.** Highest fun per line
   of code. Probably 200 lines.
4. **A player-stats derivation producer.** Pure function over
   the primary stream. No live source needed — it derives.
   Sets the schema for everyone who follows.
5. **A live-odds streamer for Polymarket CLOB.** Higher
   resolution than the current REST poll. Direct revenue path
   via the affiliate router.
6. **A leaderboard-chat → Telegram bridge.** Federates the chat
   into the bot ecosystem the platform already has.
7. **A trivia-quiz curator** that opens 30-point trivia
   questions during stoppages-in-play.
8. **A backup producer for every match the primary fails on.**
   Less glamorous, structurally important.

If you are reading this and one of those is "yes, I can ship
that", open an issue at
[github.com/0800tim/tournamental/issues](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/issues)
with the label `skill: producer`. We will reply within twenty-four
hours with the spec pointer, the test fixture you should target,
and a Drips-list slot.

## Why we are doing this

Because broadcast television's monopoly on the live-match
experience is the single largest piece of unclaimed value in
sport. The right to commentate, the right to overlay graphics,
the right to ask the audience to make a prediction, the right to
add a heatmap, the right to switch language, the right to be a
fan — these are not the broadcaster's rights to grant. They are
the fan's rights to claim. The technology now exists to claim
them, and Tournamental is the first try at packaging it up so
the claiming is easy.

If we ship this well, the FIFA World Cup 2026 will be the first
tournament in history where a meaningful share of the watching
public composes their own broadcast from a marketplace of
contributor-shipped producers running on an open platform. Even
a 0.1% market share at that scale is a category-defining
milestone for fan-owned sports media.

We are six months out from the answer. The producers are the
piece that decides it.
