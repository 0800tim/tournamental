Most football coverage on the internet is somebody else's video feed, geo-blocked and ad-stuffed. That is fine, but it has been the only option for so long that we have collectively forgotten the alternative. The match itself is a stream of events: passes, shots, fouls, substitutions. If we have those events, we have everything we need to render the match ourselves, in the browser, at any speed, with our own commentary and overlay layered on top.

That is what the Tournamental renderer is. This post walks through how it works, what shipped this weekend, the bug we found in our clock controller, and where it is going next.

## The data path

```
StatsBomb open data
        │
        ▼
apps/statsbomb-replay   (Python; converts events into spec frames)
        │
        ▼
WebSocket on :4001      (newline-delimited JSON; @vtorn/spec contract)
        │
        ▼
apps/web                (Next.js; React Three Fiber scene)
```

The contract between the producer and the renderer is the `@vtorn/spec` package. Every frame on the wire is a typed event: `KickoffEvent`, `PassEvent`, `ShotEvent`, `GoalEvent`, `SubstitutionEvent`, `CardEvent`, `WhistleEvent`, plus the periodic `StateFrame` carrying ball position and player coordinates.

The renderer subscribes to the stream and reduces it. The state frames feed the scene's transforms (smoothed with a tunable lerp so 10 Hz state updates look like 60 Hz motion); the discrete events feed the HUD overlays, the audio cues, and the score.

## The 2022 final, end-to-end

Our proof-point was the 2022 World Cup Final, **Argentina 3 to 3 France with Argentina winning on penalties**. StatsBomb open data has the entire match. The producer reads the JSON, sorts it, and emits spec frames in real time (or `time-scale=10` if you are testing) over a WebSocket on port 4001.

The renderer plays it back exactly as it happened. Score updates at 23' (Messi), 36' (Di Maria), 80' (Mbappé pen), 81' (Mbappé), 108' (Messi), 118' (Mbappé pen). Penalties: Mbappé scores; Messi scores; Coman saved by Martinez; Dybala scores; Tchouameni hits the post; Paredes scores; Kolo Muani scores; Montiel scores. Argentina win 4 to 2. Nameplate floats over the right body for the right event. Ball ends up in the goal on goals; flies toward the keeper on shots. Whistle plays at the right moment.

Once that worked end-to-end on the AR-FR final, the contract was proven and every other agent could begin to ship in parallel.

## The avatar pipeline

We made a choice early on. We could spend two years on photoreal mocap of every World Cup squad, or we could ship a stylised look that is good enough to be readable from across a room. We picked the second.

Every avatar is:

- A **procedural body GLB** — one base mesh, parameterised over height and build
- A **runtime jersey-texture generator** that paints the right kit colours, sponsor placement, and number on a fresh canvas the first time the avatar is referenced (cached after that)
- A **billboard face** — a transparent quad in front of the head with the player's photo from Wikidata, where the licence allows. If a face is unavailable, we fall back to a stylised silhouette with the shirt number floating above

This is not Madden 26. It is closer to the FM 2007 match engine had a baby with a Rocket League camera. It is **legible**: from any seat in the room you can tell that the person on the ball is wearing 10 in light blue and white, and that is enough.

The jersey textures are recomputed at runtime from a small `KitSpec` so when an unfamiliar team turns up at the World Cup we do not need any new GLB or texture asset to render them. The badge painted on the chest is a workspace asset; we ship the renderer with the colour palette only.

## The MatchStatsHUD broadcast overlay

Sky and ESPN spent twenty years tuning the broadcast overlay. We took notes.

The renderer's `MatchStatsHUD` is the equivalent layer for our 3D scene:

- **Scoreboard** at the top: kit-coloured pills, shirt-coloured score boxes, current minute (live, then HT, then 90', then 90+, then ET, then PEN with the running shootout count)
- **Scorers ticker** under the scoreboard: chronological with side-attribution; goals, own-goals (OG suffix), penalties (P suffix), and disallowed-and-restored entries
- **Stats panel**: possession (running average over the last 60 seconds), shots, on-target, corners, fouls, yellows, reds, saves; both teams side-by-side
- **Subs ticker**: rolling along the bottom edge with the standard arrow + name pattern
- **Centre goal-burst celebration**: a full-screen flash when a goal is scored with the scorer's name, time, and ground-shake

On mobile the panel collapses into a bottom drawer with tabs (Score, Scorers, Stats, Subs). Fifty-six new tests cover the components — these tend to be the things that drift.

## The clock bug we still wear scars from

Friday evening, the renderer was emitting two competing time values. The driver had a `tick` method that computed the wall-clock time inside its loop, and the scene was reading both that and a separate `controller.getTime()` that came from the spec-client. Most of the time they agreed. When they disagreed (cursor reset on a seek, or a network hiccup that paused the producer for a fraction of a second), the HUD jittered visibly and the overlay clock would tick backwards before catching up.

The fix was the architectural cleanup we should have done on day one. **`controller.getTime()` is the single source of truth.** The driver consumes it instead of computing its own. Cursor resets moved into a separate `subscribeSeek` channel so a natural advance no longer drops events through the seek path.

This is the bug you only find when the renderer plays a real match. The producer flagged a 600 ms gap during the half-time interval and that was enough to surface the divergence. Synthetic data from the mock producer is too clean to have caught it.

## The 60 fps target

Our target hardware is a **mid-range 2022 Android in a browser**. Pixel 6, Samsung A53, that level. At 60 fps with 22 player avatars, a ball, the HUD layer, and the camera following the action, this is not free.

What we cut:

- Per-pixel cloth simulation. Jerseys are baked into the GLB with the texture painted on; no swaying.
- Stadium ambient occlusion at the player level. We bake one global AO into the pitch texture and call it.
- Spectator avatars in the stands. They are painted as a single low-res billboard band per stand.

What we kept:

- Per-bone rigging on every player so the run cycle and shot animations look right
- Realtime shadows (single directional light, contact shadows)
- Sub-pixel ball motion with a 240 Hz physics step inside the renderer so the ball never tunnels through a foot

The result is a steady 60 fps on a Pixel 6 with the developer console open and a Discord call running. We will keep stretching this — the 2026 final will be played on a lot of phones at once.

## Where it goes next

The 2022-final replay was the proof-of-concept. The next thing we are wiring is the **live-data path** for the actual 2026 matches. The plan:

- A **live producer** that consumes a paid SportRadar feed and emits the same `@vtorn/spec` events to the same WebSocket. The renderer does not know the difference; the spec is the abstraction.
- A **CDN-backed stream server** so the renderer scales beyond a handful of viewers per process. Fan-out is easy; the design lives in [`docs/03-stream-server.md`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/03-stream-server.md).
- **Rewindable replay** with full scrubbing so a user can re-enter a match they missed and skim straight to the goals
- **Verified prediction overlay** so the bracket-game predictions surface during the match — your call versus the live result, in the corner of the screen

If you want to follow the engineering, the renderer's source is in [`apps/web/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/web), the producer is in [`apps/statsbomb-replay/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/statsbomb-replay), and the spec is in [`packages/spec/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/packages/spec). We will keep posting here when we ship something the casual reader would care about.
