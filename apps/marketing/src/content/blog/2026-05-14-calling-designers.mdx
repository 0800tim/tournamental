## The hook

If you are a designer, an illustrator, or someone who has ever looked at a tech product and thought "the engineer who shipped this clearly chose those colours on a Sunday afternoon", this post is for you.

The Tournamental brand exists. So does the share-card pipeline. So does the molecule view. None of them are a billion-dollar product yet.

I am asking you to help make them one. Drips will pay you a slice of platform revenue every time it earns, for as long as your work is in the codebase. This post is the long version of the call; the [pot-of-gold post](/blog/2026-05-12-pot-of-gold-or-pot-of-shit/) is the broader case for the open-source bet.

The rest of this post covers what I built, what I cannot do, what an expert designer could ship in a day, a week, or a month, how Drips pays you in USDC on a quarterly schedule, and what your specific first PR could be.

## What I built so far

The Tournamental visual surface lives in three places, and each of them is functional but visibly the work of a developer.

**The share-card system at [`packages/social-cards/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/packages/social-cards).** This is a canvas-renderer package that takes a structured payload (`{ winner: 'ARG', runner_up: 'FRA', score: '4-2 pens', user: '@tim_nz', bracket_score: 0.86 }`) and outputs PNG cards in eight aspect ratios and seven content variants: podium, goal clip still, leaderboard rank, badge earned, bracket prediction, match result, pundit badge, referral invite. The cards auto-generate on every save in the bracket app and pop up in the Save & share flow. The architecture is documented in [docs/14-clip-generation-and-social.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/14-clip-generation-and-social.md) and the rendering primitives are at [`packages/social-cards/src/canvas/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/packages/social-cards/src/canvas).

**The molecule view at [`apps/web/components/molecule/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/web/components).** This is the 3D pyramid visualisation of a forty-eight-team World Cup bracket. The user's predicted champion sits at the apex as a single gold atom. The bracket fans out below, atoms coloured by team flag, lines drawn between rounds. The whole thing is rotatable, zoomable, and shareable as a still. It is documented in [docs/24-gamification-and-virality.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/24-gamification-and-virality.md) and the doc I wrote for the blog post on it lives at [/blog/2026-05-12-tournament-as-molecule/](/blog/2026-05-12-tournament-as-molecule/).

**The brand pack at [docs/15-tournamental-brand-and-positioning.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/15-tournamental-brand-and-positioning.md).** Wordmark, taglines, colour palette, typography, tone-of-voice. The whole package is CC-BY-4.0 licensed, which means you can lift any of it into your own design system, fork it, redistribute it, or improve it back into the upstream as a pull request. The brand entity name is **Tournamental, Verified Tournament Oracle Network**. The product brand is the four-letter Tournamental. The visual mnemonic is a chevron-V (forward velocity) or a tornado-vortex (live odds swirling), or both at once.

There is also the podium graphic at [`packages/social-cards/src/cards/match-result.ts`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/packages/social-cards/src/cards/match-result.ts) (gold/silver/bronze with the user's pick highlighted), the leaderboard rank card, the badge-earned card for things like "Called the Argentina win at 25% implied probability", and the referral-invite card with a deep link to the inviter's syndicate.

### The marketing site

The marketing site you are reading this post on lives at [`apps/marketing/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/marketing). It is an [Astro](https://astro.build/) project with [MDX](https://mdxjs.com/) content collections for the blog and the press release pages. The styling is [Tailwind](https://tailwindcss.com/) at the project's published config. The hero images live under `apps/marketing/public/`. The OG images are auto-generated by a build script at `apps/marketing/scripts/build-og-cards.mjs` which uses the same `@tournamental/social-cards` package as the bracket app, so the social-share artwork for every blog post is consistent with the in-product share cards.

The pages are functional. The typography is one font at three sizes. The colour palette is the same gold-and-midnight pair as the in-product cards. The hero images are stock photos from Unsplash credited inline. The layout grid is what you get out of Tailwind's defaults.

### The OG image pipeline

The OG image pipeline is in `apps/marketing/scripts/build-og-cards.mjs`. It runs at build time. For every blog post and press release, it reads the title and the description, lays them out on a 1200×630 canvas using the `@tournamental/social-cards` primitives, and saves a PNG to `apps/marketing/public/og-cards/<slug>.png`. The Astro layout then references the PNG via the `og:image` meta tag.

The pipeline works. The output looks like every other Astro-blog OG card on the internet. A designer who cared could replace the layout with a distinctive treatment in a day; the PNG output is bound to the per-page meta tags so the change propagates automatically.

### Some illustrative examples of what is broken

Let me walk through a few specific places where my design choices break down, in case you want to skim the codebase for the work that needs doing.

**The marketing-site header.** The hero illustration on the landing page is a stock photograph of a stadium at night. It is fine. It does not say "Tournamental"; it says "I am a startup with a stadium product". A custom hero illustration in the brand voice would change the landing page's first impression entirely. The image goes in `apps/marketing/public/landing/hero.jpg` and the alt text needs to say something memorable.

**The Save & share button.** The button on the bracket app's Save flow is a Tailwind primary-button preset. It says "Save & share". It is functional. It does not feel like the most important button in the product, which it is. A bespoke button treatment, with a subtle motion on hover and a satisfying micro-animation on click, would communicate the importance.

**The leaderboard rank treatment.** The leaderboard at `apps/web/components/leaderboard/` shows ranks as plain numerals. The top three could have gold/silver/bronze treatment. The user's own rank could have a small highlight. The rank-shift animation (when a user moves up or down) could be a small upward or downward arrow with a colour pulse. None of this is implemented.

**The empty-state illustrations.** The bracket app has several empty states (no bracket saved yet, no syndicate yet, no friends yet). Today each empty state is a small icon plus a one-line label. A real designer would commission empty-state illustrations that are charming and on-brand. The illustration slots are at `apps/web/components/empty-state/` and are easy to swap.

**The favicon at small sizes.** The favicon at 32 pixels is the gold-T glyph. At 16 pixels (browser-tab size) it is a pixelated blob. A real favicon designer would author a 16-pixel-optimised glyph alongside the 32 and 64 sizes, with the right anti-aliasing for each.

### The press kit assets

The press kit lives at `apps/marketing/public/press-kit/`. It currently contains the wordmark in SVG, the favicon in PNG, three product screenshots, and a 200-word boilerplate. The press releases at `apps/marketing/src/content/press/` reference the kit. The kit is the file a journalist downloads when they email and ask for assets.

The kit has the right files. The files are not particularly polished. The wordmark is a Google Font screenshotted; the screenshots are at a default zoom level with the dev-tools sidebar visible in one of them; the boilerplate is functional but not memorable.

### The bones are there

The bones are there. The cards render. The molecule rotates. The brand book has a tagline. None of it looks like a billion-dollar product.

## What I cannot do alone

### I am a developer who picked a colour palette in thirty minutes on a Sunday

The gold I chose is a hex value I sampled off a 1980s sports trophy photograph on Wikipedia. I have no idea if it reads on a phone in direct sunlight in Lagos. I have no idea if it has the right contrast ratio against the dark mode I shipped two days later as an afterthought.

### I do not know the visual language of West African fan culture from the inside

The African Cup of Nations is one of the most loved tournaments in football and we ship in time for the next one. Today's share cards treat every team as a flag plus a colour pair plus a generic font. That is fine for the World Cup launch where every nation has an instantly recognisable flag. It is **not** fine for AFCON, where the home audience expects design that reflects fan culture, terrace songs, club colours, and visual idioms I do not speak.

### I do not know what makes a share card actually get shared

I have read [Nathan Baschez](https://every.to/p/the-most-successful-newsletter-of-2022-was-built-by-a-team-of-one) and [Andy Matuschak](https://andymatuschak.org/) and [Julie Zhuo](https://lethain.com/julie-zhuo-on-team-feedback/) on the meta-design of social objects, and I can recite the shape of a viral graphic. I cannot actually make one. The first podium card I shipped looks like every other "look I won a thing" card on the internet. It needs to look like nothing else.

### I do not have a typography opinion

The current `Inter` choice is a developer-default. Variable type pairings, optical sizing, kerning of the wordmark, the cadence of the heading-to-body ratio, all of that is upstream of where my brain stops. The marketing site has three font sizes and I chose them because they fit on the screen. The bracket app has the same three font sizes. The share cards have the same three. There is no documented type scale; there is no documented line-height system; there is no documented per-language fallback for languages whose script does not fit comfortably in Inter.

### I do not understand motion

The animated podium template for short-form video does not exist; today it is a still PNG. A real motion designer would build that template in [After Effects](https://www.adobe.com/products/aftereffects.html) or [Lottie](https://airbnb.io/lottie/) and the engagement rate on TikTok would double overnight. I do not know how to do this and I do not know who I am supposed to learn it from. The clip pipeline at [`apps/clip-pipeline/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/apps/clip-pipeline) could consume Lottie JSON directly, but the JSON does not exist because I have not authored it.

### I do not have a colour-system theory

The brand pack's palette is a primary (`#F4C430` "tournament gold"), an accent (`#0B2545` "midnight"), and two neutrals. That is it. No tertiary. No tonal ladder. No accessible-pair documentation. A real colour system would have eight to twelve shades of each, mapped to WCAG-AA contrast pairings, with a documented dark-mode inversion. I do not have any of this. The dark mode I shipped is a CSS hack that flips the lightness on the primary and the neutrals; the share cards do not respect it because they render through the `@tournamental/social-cards` canvas pipeline which has no dark-mode awareness.

### I do not know how to re-skin the molecule view for non-football tournaments

The atom textures are flag colours. The bond textures are league-tier lines. None of this generalises to the NFL, the NRL, the IPL, or the ATP. The molecule is meant to be the **shape** Tournamental uses for any tournament, but the visual translation to non-football is not done.

### I do not have an icon set

The bracket app uses [Lucide](https://lucide.dev/) icons at the default settings. The icons are functional. They are not distinctive. Tournamental does not have a custom icon set; we have not commissioned one; I would not know how to brief one. A designer who shipped a 20-icon custom set in the brand voice would change how the entire bracket app feels and the per-icon cost is tractable for a focused weekend.

### I do not have a sound-design pass

The product makes sounds. The lock-prediction confirmation makes a small chime. The save-and-share flow makes a different chime. The kickoff lockout warning beeps once. The sounds were generated in [Bfxr](https://www.bfxr.net/) on a Sunday afternoon. They are placeholder sounds. A real sound designer could replace them with a coherent five-sound palette in a day and the product would feel ten percent more like a product.

### The marketing site looks like it was built in a week

Because it was built in a week. The blog component is fine. The landing page is fine. The press-release pages are fine. None of it is **distinctive**. Every other Apache-2.0 startup landing page on the internet uses the same Tailwind preset I am using. A designer who cared could change that in ten days and it would feed back into every share card, every blog hero image, and every social post for the next year.

## The opportunity

The visual surface is small enough to actually fit in a designer's head, and large enough that improvements compound across the whole product. Every share card that ships in your new template propagates to thousands of users in the first tournament cycle.

### The big picture

The visual fidelity ladder, from where we are today to "this brand is unmistakable":

- **Level 0**, the developer-defaults. Inter type, a Tailwind palette, a Sunday-afternoon gold. We are here.
- **Level 1**, the coherent. A proper type system, a tonal-ladder colour palette, a small custom icon set, a wordmark with optical kerning. Two to three months of focused work.
- **Level 2**, the distinctive. A motion language, a custom illustration set, a sound palette, a regional variant pack. Six months of focused work plus iteration.
- **Level 3**, the iconic. The brand identity that people recognise from across the room. Multiple years.

We need to ship Level 1 in time for the World Cup. Level 2 by the second tournament cycle. Level 3 is the long-term ambition.

### A category overview

Design contributions cluster into five buckets:

1. **Visual identity.** The wordmark, the logo, the icon set, the typography system, the colour ladder.
2. **Share card design.** Per-card-type templates, per-region variants, per-event template variations.
3. **Marketing site design.** Landing page, blog typography, press kit, OG card design.
4. **Motion design.** Animated share cards, intro animations, micro-interactions, transition curves.
5. **Sound design.** UI chimes, transition sounds, the celebratory pop on a saved bracket.

Each bucket is mostly independent of the others. A typography contributor can ship into bucket 1 without touching bucket 2; a motion designer can ship into bucket 4 without touching anything else. Pick the bucket you have the most experience in.

### A note on tooling

The project does not require any specific design tool. If you author in [Figma](https://www.figma.com/), the project has a Figma file at `https://www.figma.com/community/file/tournamental-brand` (link goes live on the public reveal) that mirrors the brand pack. If you author in [Sketch](https://www.sketch.com/), Penpot, or Adobe XD, that is fine; you export to SVG and the SVG goes in `apps/marketing/public/brand/` or `packages/social-cards/src/assets/`. The reviewer agent reads the SVG; the SVG drives the product. The tool is your choice.

For motion work, the rendering pipeline expects [Lottie JSON](https://airbnb.io/lottie/#/) because Lottie is the only motion format that runs in our headless Chromium clip renderer without ffmpeg shenanigans. After Effects → Bodymovin works. We can probably add [Rive](https://rive.app/) support if a designer comes with a compelling case.

### The licensing path

The whole brand pack is CC-BY-4.0 licensed at [docs/LICENSE-DOCS](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/LICENSE-DOCS), which means contributions feed back into a **public design system** the wider open-source web can use. If your design language ends up in Tournamental and you also want to use the same system for a separate project, you can. The licence is set up for that. You credit the source, you keep the licence, you ship.

If you contribute design work, you retain the underlying copyright on the work. The project gets a broad licence to use, redistribute, and modify. The DCO sign-off on the commit captures this. If you want to maintain stricter control of the licence (for example, you want the work to be available to Tournamental but not to commercial forks), the brand-asset registration path is documented in `docs/15-tournamental-brand-and-positioning.md` and is a separate conversation. The default is CC-BY-4.0 and most contributors take the default.

### A design system, in practice

The phrase "design system" is overused. What it means concretely for Tournamental is:

- **A documented type scale.** Six sizes, with line-heights and tracking values. Stored as Tailwind tokens at `apps/web/tailwind.config.ts` and as CSS custom properties at `apps/web/styles/typography.css`.
- **A documented colour palette.** Eight shades per primary, accent, and neutral. Each shade has a documented WCAG-AA pairing. Stored as Tailwind tokens and CSS custom properties.
- **A documented spacing scale.** Tailwind's default 4-pixel-multiples works fine; the documentation makes the choices explicit.
- **A documented motion-curve library.** Three easing curves: a sharp-out (for clicks), a smooth-in (for hovers), an emphatic-bounce (for celebratory moments). Defined as cubic-bezier values stored as CSS custom properties.
- **A documented icon set.** Twenty icons in the brand voice. Stored as SVG components at `apps/web/components/icons/`.

This is the level of system a Level-1 brand pack ships. Tournamental does not have it yet. A designer who shipped this in four weeks would land the foundational work that every other design contribution depends on.

### Why design contributions compound

Unlike code contributions, design contributions compound in a particular way. A new card type ships in the codebase; every future bracket save uses it. A typography system ships; every future blog post inherits it. A motion template ships; every future clip is built around it. The marginal cost of each future use is zero. The value compounds across every user, forever.

This is also why design decisions are harder to reverse than code decisions. A typography system that does not work has to be ripped out, not refactored. The reviewer agent is mostly aware of this and gives design PRs a longer review cycle than code PRs of equivalent size. Plan for a few days between PR open and PR merge on a big design change; the design council (Tim plus two human design contributors, soon-to-be three or four as the team grows) reviews the change against the brand pack and the existing system before merge.

## How Drips pays you

Designers get the same revenue-share programme as code contributors. The whole system is documented in [docs/19-open-source-and-contributor-revenue.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/docs/19-open-source-and-contributor-revenue.md). The short version, with the design-specific bits called out:

### The Drips Network primer

Tournamental's revenue routes through [Drips Network](https://www.drips.network/) to a list of contributor wallets. Drips is the same protocol that the [Ethereum Foundation](https://ethereum.foundation/), [Radworks](https://radworks.org/), the [Filecoin Foundation](https://fil.org/), [ENS](https://ens.domains/), [UNICEF](https://www.unicef.org/) and [Scroll](https://scroll.io/) use to stream funding to open-source contributors. It is on-chain. It is transparent. You can read the contract, fork it, propose a better split formula, or audit the multisig that controls the foundation's treasury wallet.

### The treasury policy

The Tournamental Foundation deposits a fixed percentage of net affiliate revenue and other platform monetization lanes into a Drips treasury wallet, paid in USDC. The current split is **thirty percent to contributors, fifty percent to the operating reserve (capped at twelve months), ten percent to public goods, ten percent to strategic reserve**. Once the operating reserve cap is full, more flows to contributors. The split rebalances annually based on actual revenue.

Every paid affiliate click that traces back to a Tournamental product surface, including share cards, the molecule view, the marketing site, the Save & share page, routes a slice through Drips to the contributors who shipped that surface. If your podium template ends up driving a measurable lift in share-to-conversion on TikTok, that lift shows up on the affiliate report and a fraction of the revenue routes to your wallet address.

### The scoring formula, for design work

Design contributions are scored under the same rules as code. The doc 19 scoring table credits design contributions specifically as:

- **Significant features** (anything that ships as a new card type, a new view, or a major brand revision) earn a flat per-feature bounty at acceptance.
- **Per-PR LoC weight** applies to the design system code (palette files, token files, SVG sources, motion JSON) the same way it does to product code.
- **Major design contributions (specs, RFCs)** earn a per-RFC bounty when accepted. If you write the South Asia regional-flavour brand pack as an RFC and the council accepts it, that is a paid milestone.
- **Reviews of others' PRs** earn at twenty percent of the original PR score. If you are a senior designer reviewing other designers' work, you earn a slice of theirs too.

### Decay and dilution

Decay is the same: a contribution made today is worth one hundred percent next quarter, eighty the quarter after, then sixty, forty, twenty, zero. Forces ongoing contribution. Prevents the first-mover from rent-seeking on the original brand pack forever.

For brand-pack contributions specifically, the decay schedule is gentler than the standard schedule. A coherent typography system that ships in Q1 2026 retains its score at 100% for two quarters before decaying, because the value compounds across every piece of content that uses it. This is an exception documented in doc 19 and applies to "foundational design work" generally.

### A worked example of the payout flow

Imagine you ship the AFCON podium variant in your first PR. The PR scores 800 points (per-feature bounty plus per-line credit). The quarterly score sheet for Q1 2026 finalises with your score at 1.2 percent of the total.

If the Drip List receives 30,000 USDC for the quarter (a notional number; the actual depends on platform revenue, which we publish openly), your share is 360 USDC for Q1, streamed continuously over the quarter into your wallet. You can claim at any time, or let it accrue. The Drips contract handles the streaming math.

In Q2, your Q1 contribution decays to 80 percent of its original score. If you also ship the colour-ladder PR in Q2, your Q2 contribution adds on top. Your total score across both quarters is the sum, weighted by the decay. The quarterly distribution adjusts.

If you stop contributing, your stream tapers across five quarters and ends. If the project shuts down, the stream ends sooner. The model is deliberately built on ongoing contribution.

### The wallet integration

The wallet integration is the same as the rest of the contributor programme. You register your GitHub username with an Ethereum address at the contributor sign-up page. Sanctions screening runs at payout time. The quarterly score sheet is published as a public Markdown file in the `payouts/` directory of the repo. The Drip List is multisig-controlled by the Tournamental Foundation. The contract is transparent and on-chain. You can read it. You can fork it.

If you would rather contribute under your studio's name than your own GitHub identity, the contributor registration page accepts a multi-signer setup. Your studio's wallet receives the stream and you split it internally however you want.

### Comparable projects

If you want a sanity-check on whether "open source plus revenue share via Drips for designers" is a credible model, the precedents are:

- **[Mozilla Foundation](https://foundation.mozilla.org/)**, where the brand identity is open and design contributions come through a documented system.
- **[Penpot](https://penpot.app/)**, the open-source design tool, whose contributor community includes both engineers and designers under the same MPL-2.0 framework.
- **[Inkscape](https://inkscape.org/)**, the open-source illustration tool, whose icon set is community-contributed under the GPL.
- **[Radworks](https://radworks.org/)**, which has streamed design grants via Drips alongside engineering grants.

Tournamental is positioning *alongside* these structurally, with the brand pack as the public design system and the Drips list as the revenue path for ongoing design work.

### Opting out is fine

You do not have to opt in. The licence is CC-BY-4.0 either way. Some designers prefer not to receive payments for tax, personal, or philosophical reasons, and that is fine. The point is the option exists, and it is not vapour.

## How to start

1. Clone the repo: `git clone https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental`. You do not need to run the AR-FR demo for design work; you just need the brand pack and the share-card package.
2. 3. Look at the existing share-card variants in [`packages/social-cards/src/cards/`](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/tree/main/packages/social-cards/src/cards). Each variant is a TypeScript module that exports a `render(payload, ctx)` function. The test fixtures are in `packages/social-cards/test/fixtures/`. The build script renders sample cards to `packages/social-cards/test/output/` so you can see what changes look like.

5. For motion work, the rendering pipeline expects [Lottie JSON](https://airbnb.io/lottie/#/) at the moment because Lottie is the only motion format that runs in our headless Chromium clip renderer without ffmpeg shenanigans. After Effects → Bodymovin works. We can probably add Rive support if a designer comes with a compelling case.

If you have never contributed to an open-source repo before, that is fine. The session-note discipline at [AGENT-PROMPTS.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/AGENT-PROMPTS.md) is the same for designers as for engineers, but a designer's session note is mostly notes-to-self: "I chose this gold because it survives at 30 ppi on a Pixel 5 in noon sun". The point of the discipline is that the next contributor reads your note and learns from your reasoning.

### What good design PRs look like

- Does the PR include a before/after screenshot?
- Does the change align with the brand pack at `docs/15-tournamental-brand-and-positioning.md`?
- Does the change pass the WCAG-AA contrast check (run via `pnpm a11y` in the marketing app)?
- Does the change include the asset in SVG (vector formats preferred) where applicable?
- Is the session note legible?

Five checks. Most design PRs pass on the first review.

### What bad PRs look like

The reviewer agent rejects design PRs that:

- Are missing the before/after screenshots.
- Use a colour value not from the brand pack without a documented exception.
- Use a non-vector format (JPG, PNG) where a vector (SVG) is feasible.
- Fail the WCAG-AA contrast check.
- Mix unrelated changes (a typography refresh plus a new card variant in one PR).
- Touch the brand pack itself without a discussion thread opened in advance.

If the reviewer agent rejects your PR, the rejection comment names the rule and links to the relevant doc. Fix the issue, push a new commit, re-request review.

### Frequently asked questions

**Do I need to use Figma?**

No. Use whatever design tool you prefer. The repo accepts SVG, PNG, and Lottie JSON. The Figma file is a convenience, not a requirement.

**Can I ship a design that does not match the brand pack?**

You can propose a brand-pack update in the same PR. The design council reviews the change and either accepts the new direction or asks you to revise. The brand pack is not sacred but it is the published baseline; departing from it requires justification.

**What if I want to redesign the whole product?**

Open a Discussion thread first. A whole-product redesign is a multi-month project and the council will want to discuss the direction before you spend the time. The brand pack is the right starting point; the design council is the right audience.

**Can I work with a co-author?**

Yes. The reviewer agent supports multi-author PRs via the standard `Co-authored-by` trailer in the commit message. Both authors are credited on the score sheet.

**What if my design only works in dark mode?**

The product supports both light and dark mode. Designs that only render in one mode are rejected at review. The dark-mode CSS lives in `apps/web/styles/dark.css`; the share-card dark-mode renderer is on the roadmap and is one of the open design RFCs.

**What happens if I disagree with the design council on a review?**

The dispute path is the same as for code: open a Discussion thread, lay out the case, the council adjudicates. If the disagreement is genuine and the council is split, Tim has the casting vote until the council expands to seven members (which is the documented threshold for full majority-rule).

Two opt out of Drips and contribute as public-good work. The bracket app, the share cards, the marketing site, and the molecule view all gain a coherent visual identity by the time the World Cup kicks off, and the rest of the platform inherits the identity for the rest of 2026.

If that happens, every user who sees a Tournamental share card on TikTok or Reels recognises the brand. If it does not, the share cards keep looking like every other "look I won a thing" card on the internet and the visual story Tournamental tells about itself stays smaller than it could be.

### A final honest admission

I do not know what the right visual identity for Tournamental is. The brand pack is my best guess. I have looked at the visual identities of [ESPN's Bracket Challenge](https://www.espn.com/), [Yahoo Sports' fantasy products](https://sports.yahoo.com/fantasy/), [Dream11](https://www.dream11.com/), and a few other tournament-prediction products, and I have a sense for what works and what does not in the category. I have not been able to translate that sense into a brand pack that I am confident in. The thirty-minute Sunday-afternoon palette is the literal artefact of that gap.

If you bring a stronger sense of what the brand should be, the brand pack updates around you. The published version is a baseline. The version that ships in twelve months is the version a real designer made.

### What the project does not need from a design contributor

To save you time, the things that are **not** in scope for design contributions:

- **A complete tear-down of the brand pack.** The brand pack updates incrementally. A complete tear-down requires an RFC and council review and is not the first PR you ship.
- **Replacing the Astro stack with a different framework.** The marketing site stays Astro. Design contributions live within Astro.
- **A non-Apache, non-CC-BY licence.** Design contributions ship under CC-BY-4.0 by default. Other licences require a discussion before the PR.
- **A redesign of the bracket-game mechanics.** The mechanics are owned by the product team; the visual treatment of the mechanics is owned by design. Stay on the visual-treatment side.
- **Custom typography that requires a paid font licence.** Tournamental pays for [Adobe Fonts](https://fonts.adobe.com/) and the [Google Fonts](https://fonts.google.com/) catalogue is free. Paid foundry-specific licences require strategic-reserve budget approval first.

### Frequently asked questions, expanded

**Can I use AI tools to generate parts of my design contribution?**

Yes. The reviewer agent itself is an AI; AI-assisted design is fine. The disclosure rule (from `docs/19-open-source-and-contributor-revenue.md` Anti-Gaming section) is that contributions credited to a contributor are expected to be substantively your work, not just an LLM prompt with no editorial pass. If you use AI tools as a starting point, ship the human-edited result.

**What if I want to commission a third-party studio for a project that ships under my contributor account?**

That works. The contributor wallet on the Drips list can route a share to a third-party. Document the arrangement in your session note. The council sees it and treats your contribution as the deliverable; how you distribute the payment is your choice.

**What if I want my design to be paid as a fixed-price contract instead of a Drips stream?**

The strategic-reserve line item handles fixed-price contracts. Email `tim@tournamental.com` with the scope and the budget. Most designers prefer Drips because it tracks upside; a few prefer fixed-price for cashflow reasons. Both are open.

**Can I work on multiple PRs at once?**

Yes. The repo uses git worktrees for parallel work. The reviewer agent treats each PR independently. The only constraint is that PRs touching the same brand-pack file may need to rebase; the orchestrator usually catches this and warns in the PR comments.

**Can I rebrand the design in a fork?**

The code is Apache 2.0; the docs are CC-BY-4.0. The Tournamental brand assets (the name, the wordmark, the logo) are not open and you cannot use them in a fork. Everything else, including the share-card templates, the molecule view, the marketing site styling, is yours to fork and rebrand. The brand-reservation policy is in [TRADEMARK.md](https://github.com/0800tim/tournamental/blob/main/TRADEMARK.md).

**Will the brand evolve over time?**

Yes. The Level-1 brand pack ships in May 2026. The Level-2 evolution is a 2027 project. The Level-3 ambition is a 2028+ project. Designers who contribute to the initial pack are positioning to lead the evolution.

### How the marketing site relates to the bracket app

The bracket app at `apps/web/` and the marketing site at `apps/marketing/` share the brand pack but are technically independent. The Astro marketing site is a static-site generation tool; the Next.js bracket app is a dynamic application with server-side routes and live data.

For design contributions, this matters because: the marketing site can use rich illustration and motion at a level the bracket app cannot afford (the bracket app's bundle budget is tighter); the bracket app needs design that holds up at 60 frames per second during the renderer scene; the share cards live in a third place (a canvas-rendered pipeline that produces PNG files at build time and at runtime).

A designer ships either app-side or marketing-side depending on the change. Some contributions cross the boundary (the wordmark, the colour palette, the type system). The brand pack at `docs/15-` lives at the project level and is the source of truth for both.

### Accessibility, in practice

Every design PR is checked against WCAG-AA contrast. The check runs as `pnpm a11y` in the marketing app and is part of the reviewer-agent checklist. Failures block the merge unless the contributor documents a specific exception.

The accessibility goals beyond contrast:

- **Keyboard navigation** on every interactive element. The bracket app supports keyboard-only flow through the bracket-edit interface; the share flow does too.
- **Screen-reader support** on every interactive element. The aria-label and aria-describedby attributes are part of the standard component pattern.
- **Reduced-motion respect** on every animated element. The `prefers-reduced-motion` media query disables non-essential motion.
- **Colour-blind safety** on every chart and visualisation. The molecule view atom colours are intentionally chosen to be distinguishable across the major colour-blindness types; the share cards do not rely on red-green distinction alone.

If your design touches any of these, the reviewer agent's check is the first line. The council's review is the second.

### How design council reviews actually work

When you open a design PR, the council review process is:

1. The reviewer agent runs the mechanical checks (contrast, format, brand-pack alignment).
2. The PR is added to the council's weekly review queue.
3. Each council member (currently three of us) leaves a comment on the visual quality.
4. If the comments are positive, Tim merges. If there is a substantive concern, the comment thread continues until resolved.
5. The PR merges or is held back for revision.

The cadence is weekly because design changes have downstream impact and the council wants to consider the impact carefully. Smaller fixes (a contrast adjustment, a typo in the wordmark) skip the council and merge on the reviewer agent's approval alone.

### One specific moment I want to flag

Inside the bracket app, the moment a user hits **Save & share** on their first bracket is the single most important interaction in the product. The user has spent five-to-fifteen minutes building their bracket. The save is the payoff. The share is the multiplier.

Today's design of that moment is: a flash to a static podium card, a brief animation, then the share-card preview with a "share to X" button bar at the bottom. It works. It does not feel like the most important interaction in the product. A designer who landed on the right treatment for that single moment would change the platform's growth curve in a measurable way.

I do not know what the right treatment is. A reveal sequence with a sense of weight, a sound cue that lands the moment, a visual treatment that makes the user want to screenshot the reveal before they share it. Designers who have shipped this kind of moment in other products (the [Spotify Wrapped](https://www.spotify.com/wrapped) reveal sequence is the obvious reference) probably have an instinct here. I would love to see what they bring.
